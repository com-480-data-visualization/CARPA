The Story in 229 Million Ratings

A book rating is a uniquely honest data point. Nobody finishes a 400-page novel for algorithmic visibility. When millions of such signals are aggregated over time, they trace the emotional currents of a population.

We analyzed the UCSD Book Graph — 2.36 million books, 229 million interactions, and 15.7 million full-text reviews — to make those currents visible.

Do Reviewers Rate More Critically Over Time?

We estimate rating drift with a longitudinal cohort of Goodreads reviewers from the UCSD Book Graph. The analysis includes users with at least 30 dated ratings, orders each user's observations by read_at when available and date_added otherwise, and then computes the mean rating at each chronological position in the review sequence.

Across 15.2 million usable timestamped ratings from 94,199 qualifying reviewers, the mean rating falls from 4.10 at the first dated rating to 3.84 at position 250, a -0.26-star aggregate shift. This pattern is reported as descriptive evidence of platform-level rating behavior, not as a causal claim that individual readers become inherently more critical.

What We Want to Read vs. What We Actually Read

Goodreads separates intention from follow-through: users can shelf a book as "to-read" long before it becomes an actual read. We compare each genre's share of to-read shelf activity with its share of read interactions, then sort by the percentage-point gap.

Young Adult shows the largest intent-heavy gap in the current aggregate, while Children and Comics & Graphic flip the pattern: they are more often logged as read than parked for later. This is a proxy for behavior, not a completion rate, because Goodreads shelves overlap and only capture users who chose to log their books.
